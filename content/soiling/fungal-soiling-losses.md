## Field Signature

Biological soiling includes persistent surface contamination such as fungal, algal, lichen, pollen-biofilm, or organic growth that reduces light transmission through the module glass. It can look like ordinary soiling in production data, but it often responds differently to rain and may require a more deliberate cleaning method.

The production signature is a persistent reduction in irradiance-normalized output. Losses may be strongest in humid areas, near vegetation, around water bodies, or in zones where modules stay damp for longer portions of the day.

## Loss Mechanisms

Organic films and biological deposits reduce transmitted irradiance and can create nonuniform current loss across modules or strings. When the contamination is patchy, the resulting mismatch can be more severe than a uniform transmittance reduction would suggest.

Because biological deposits may not wash off during normal rain events, they can create a baseline loss that survives the weather resets expected in conventional dust soiling models.

## Diagnostic Workflow

Start by comparing long-term normalized production with cleaning, rainfall, and humidity patterns. If output does not recover after rain but improves after targeted cleaning, persistent contamination is more likely than ordinary dry dust accumulation.

Confirm with field inspection or high-resolution imagery. Samples may be needed when cleaning method, warranty responsibility, or recurrence risk depends on whether the deposit is biological, mineral, industrial, or agricultural in origin.

## Engineering Notes

The cited PVPMC references address operational soiling and temporal-resolution effects rather than fungus specifically. Use them as support for the broader soiling-loss workflow, then rely on site evidence to distinguish biological contamination from dust, mineral scale, pollen, or sensor error.
